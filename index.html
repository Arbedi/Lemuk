CODING

I have always struggled to find meaning as a single element of a whole that is composed of ~7.8 billion others. In examining this discomfort, I've noticed that I can fill this void with fulfilment I gain from investing time and energy into projects I believe to have potential to have a positive impact on the world.


I started learning how to code after realizing that it will be an essential skill to have in my engineering journey, and to build more complex systems that will be able to receive instructions.


As someone accustomed to writing mostly prose, my initial experience with programming languages was anything but seamless. For sometime, I was stuck in the "How to Learn How to Code" rabbit hole, gradually feeling more overwhelmed by the scope of the field. This went on until I finally commited myself towards being more systematic when it comes to my approach to learn programming languages. I started intently following the well-thought-out lesson plan of the courseware I enrolled in, and conscientiously tried to apply what I learned by creating projects with them. This simple methodology served me well, and accelerated the pace at which I absored the languages drastically.


Hence, whenever a friend openly voices out their desire to learn how to code, I would always advise them to stop waiting for externally induced motivation from YouTube videos and the likes. I also tell them that they shouldn't mull over the decision of "what language they should learn." Instead, they should start as soon as they can and atomize the syllabus they're following.


While on the topic, I did have a couple of friends ask me which online courses I took, so I listed them out in a Notion page I made here.

HEALTHY LIVING

I have always been facinated by the concept of achieving optimal efficiency. This interest was sparked when I first heard about the concept of ego-depletion that was popularized by Roy Baumeister. It was just so intuitive and appealing to the popular assumption that focus was a finite asset. Ego depletion was later debunked by Prof. Carol Dweck and colleagues' research. Which then got me even more invested into finding out how to eek out efficiency in the things we do.


During this time, I started allocating some of this enthusiasm towards performance into my health. I started abiding to a daily routine of exercise and trying out numerous methods of dieting (which was pretty hard to stick to alone, since it was pretty hard to convince my 15 y.o. friends to also buy into the program). The one form of dieting that I subscribed to was the "Ketogenic Diet." It's a bit too complex for someone with no qualifications, such as I, to explain here while giving the approach justice. Glazing past that, it worked for me! I got results. But I also developed symptoms of body dismorphia and meant I has to reject a slice of orange a friend offered me because I was afraid it will tip me off my daily balance and break my state of ketosis.


I had to put in work to address the issues I had stemming from my days as a greenhorn in the fitness journey —- but I came out of it as a more disciplined and confident person. Although I do grow a bit anxious about the spike in people interested in getting with the program for understandable, yet could be wrong reasons when taken to the extreme (e.g. seeking external validation, to project a commanding personality, hyper-fixated emulation, etc.), I'm hopeful that there are enough people who started out having the same thoughts, as I myself also had, who are ready to steer them towards a more heathy approach to self-betterment.

PharmaSee

This was the project my group came up in my first Hackathon. This particular Hackathon was hosted by Impact Hub and was themed towards healthcare services.


We wanted to create an app that would serve as a local dictionary, magazine, and marketplace for medicine to try and address the health literary problem in the Philippines. I had no involvement with the coding project, that was handled by the other members of our group who were WAY more skilled than I was. I had the responsibility of organizing and assigning tasks, handling the business aspect, and making our presentation.


We were a bit stressed because of the time constraints and our noviceness. Nonetheless, we had so much fun building the project, and got to meet so many inspiring and amazing people. Although we didn't win anything, we learned so much that we were eager to jump to another one right after!

Pythonic Conic Graph Transformer

Our professor for the course CCC101 - Computer Programming 1 announced pretty early on in the semester that our final project for his subject could be anything coded with Python. I was struggling to decide on what I should make, but I knew I wanted it to have a GUI.


A few months has passed and the time has come for me to start working on something — anything really. The idea of making a Graph Transformer came to me while I was trying to teach one of my cousins the relationship between the conic section equations and their graphs. I did a quick google seach for something like that online and couldn't find one. Of course, I could just use desmos but I wanted something more intuitive. Thus, I started working on a program that would require only inputs that would be easier to understand for my younger cousin.


(As an added bonus, I was able to find some gaps in my memory on the subject and managed to fill them up)


Our professor did have a fair critique on it, which he pointed out after the presentation: "there is no grid on the graph." Indeed, this was noticed during the creation phase but matplotlib proved to be the most stable module to use with Tkinter, so I went with that.
